Investing Basics

The key is to buy low and sell high! Try to buy stocks that, based on trends displayed in graphs, you expect will go up in price after you buy them. You also want to develop a diverse portfolio. This means that you should aim to have investments that are in unrelated business areas, as well as a good mix of investment types (stocks/bonds). Finally, here at Prudential, we want our investments to be sustainable, meaning they have Environmental, Social, and/or Governance benefits, forming the acronym ESG. To learn more about ESG Integration and the different categories, navigate over to the ESG Integration tab.

ESG Integration

ESG stands for Environmental, Social, and Governance. Environmental refers to mitigating climate change and harm to the environment, Social refers to ensuring financial security for all and taking care of employees, and Governance refers to always driving company actions with Prudential's core values. Whenever possible, ESG factors should be considered when determining where to invest our money and how to analyze security, since ESG factors can heavily affect asset returns in the long run. Though return on investment is important, Prudential separates itself from the rest by also considering ESG factors to be vital.

Sustainable Investments

Here are the types of sustainable investments, as well as examples of each type:

  1. Externally labeled ESG debt: Green bonds, Social bonds, Sustainability bonds, Sustainability-linked bonds
  2. Green Investments: Renewable energy, energy efficiency, green buildings, clean transportation, environmentally sustainable management of living natural resources and land use
  3. Social Investments: Basic infrastructure, essential services, affordable housing, financial inclusion, food security and sustainable food systems, employment generation.

Sustainable Investments

In order to protect and further Prudential's ESG integration and investment policies, please refrain from investing in the following areas:

  1. Thermal Coal: Investments in mining/utility companies that generate more than 25% of their revenue from thermal coal. Companies with strategies for transitioning into low carbon, as well as the green bonds of the restricted companies, may be granted an exception.
  2. Controversial Weapons: Companies that directly manufacture and produce cluster munitions, anti-personnel mines, biological weapons, or chemical weapons.
